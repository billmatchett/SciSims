Einstein Train Thought Experiment Simulator

Explore the relativity of simultaneity using a train, two lightning strikes and observers in different frames of reference.

Learning Intention

Students will understand that two distant events judged simultaneous in one frame of reference may not be judged simultaneous in another moving frame.

Success Criteria

Students can compare the platform and train frames, identify which light signal is received first, and explain why simultaneity is frame-dependent.

Student Challenge

  1. Set the timing offset to 0.0. What does the platform observer conclude?
  2. Increase the train speed from 0.20c to 0.80c. How does the train passenger's judgement change?
  3. Use the timeline to explain why simultaneity is not absolute.
  4. Find a timing offset where the train passenger judges the events as simultaneous. What does the platform observer judge?

Teacher Notes

This simulator models Einstein's train thought experiment as a conceptual introduction to the relativity of simultaneity.

  • Key idea: simultaneity depends on the observer's frame of reference when events are separated in space.
  • Platform frame: two distant lightning strikes can be set to occur at the same time.
  • Train frame: the train passenger assigns different times to the distant strikes because their frame is moving relative to the platform frame.
  • Formula used: Δt′ = γ(Δt − vΔx/c²), using c = 1 simulation units.
  • Teaching caution: this is a simplified visual model. The wavefronts and spacing are exaggerated so students can see the logic of the thought experiment.
