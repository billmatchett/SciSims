Gravity Slingshot Challenge

Launch a probe past a planet and try to reach the target zone. Adjust launch angle, launch speed and gravity strength to investigate how gravitational force changes the path of a moving object.

Learning Intention

Students will understand that gravity can change the direction and speed of a moving object.

Success Criteria

Students can adjust launch variables, collect trial data and explain how gravity affects a probe’s trajectory.

Slingshot flight path

Adjust the launch settings, then press Launch probe. Try to guide the probe into the green target zone.
Model note: This is a simplified 2D gravitational deflection model. The planet is fixed in place, so the probe’s path is bent by gravity. A real gravity assist also depends on the planet’s motion through space and can change the spacecraft’s speed relative to the Sun.

Student challenge

  • Complete five launches using the Earth-like planet scenario.
  • Change only one variable at a time: angle, speed or gravity strength.
  • Record which settings produce the closest approach to the target.
  • Identify the independent variable, dependent variable and controlled variables.
  • Explain how the planet’s gravity changed the probe’s path.
  • Evaluate one strength and one limitation of this model.

Teacher notes

This game models how gravity can curve the path of a moving object. Students adjust launch angle, speed and gravity strength, then observe how the probe’s trajectory changes.

Suggested investigation question: How do launch angle and launch speed affect the path of a probe near a planet? The independent variable could be launch angle, launch speed or gravity strength. Dependent variables include closest distance to the target, success or failure, flight time and maximum speed. Controlled variables include scenario, target position, starting position and number of trials.

This is a simplified gravitational deflection model. A real gravity assist involves a moving planet and can transfer energy between the planet and spacecraft. Real trajectories also involve three-dimensional motion, changing planetary positions, orbital speed, mass, distance and conservation of momentum.
