Reaction and Accuracy Target Lab

Click the target as many times as possible before the timer runs out. Use your results to investigate reaction time, focus, repeated trials and whether practice improves performance.

Learning Intention

Students will understand that reaction speed and accuracy can be investigated by collecting repeated trial data.

Success Criteria

Students can collect score data, calculate simple averages, identify patterns and explain how variables may affect performance.

Student challenge

  • Complete five trials using the same game length.
  • Record whether your score improves, decreases or stays about the same.
  • Calculate your mean score.
  • Identify one variable that could affect the results, such as practice, tiredness, distractions or device type.
  • Explain whether this is a fair test and how it could be improved.

Teacher notes

This simulation can be used as a short Working Scientifically activity. Students can investigate whether repeated practice improves performance, whether distractions reduce accuracy, or whether different input devices affect scores. It supports discussion of independent variables, dependent variables, controlled variables, repeated trials, mean results, reliability and fair testing.

Suggested investigation question: How does practice affect the number of targets clicked in 30 seconds? The independent variable could be trial number or practice condition. The dependent variable is the score or hits per second. Controlled variables include game length, device, target size, screen size, sitting position and level of distraction.
